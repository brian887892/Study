‚ú® The Function of the Lymphatic System (The "Three Pillars") ‚ú®

The overall function of the lymphatic system is threefold:

  1. Maintain Blood Volume & Fluid Balance: It picks up excess interstitial fluid (about 3 liters per day) and leaked plasma proteins from the interstitial environment and returns it to the bloodstream. This maintains blood volume and pressure. This process also promotes mixing of the ECF divisions (plasma and interstitial fluid) and enhances the delivery of substances to cells.
  2. Transport Lipids: It transports dietary fats and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) absorbed from the GI tract via specialized lymphatic capillaries called lacteals.
  3. Immunity & Body Defense: It houses the cells, tissues, and organs responsible for defending the body against environmental threats (pathogens) and internal threats (e.g., cancer cells). The lymphatic capillaries intercept invading microbes when collecting interstitial fluid, which is then exposed to immune cells in the lymph nodes.

‚ú® Anatomy Overview: Key Components ‚ú®

The lymphatic system consists of lymph, lymphatic vessels, and lymphatic tissues/organs.

üîµ ¬∑ Lymph: The fluid of the lymphatic system.
  • Composition: Similar to plasma but with less protein. It contains water, electrolytes, waste products, and leukocytes (mostly lymphocytes). It may also contain fats (making it appear milky, then called chyle) and microbes.
üü¢ ¬∑ Lymphatic Vessels (Lymphatics): A network of transport tubes.
  • Pathway: They begin blindly in the tissues as lymphatic capillaries and end at the subclavian veins, where lymph is returned to the bloodstream. Unlike blood vessels, this is a one-way system.
üü† ¬∑ Lymphatic Tissues: Collections of lymphocytes and other immune cells (macrophages, dendritic cells) in connective tissue. They do not have a connective tissue capsule.
  • Diffuse Lymphatic Tissue: Scattered lymphocytes in the mucous membranes lining the respiratory, digestive, urinary, and reproductive tracts. This is collectively called MALT (Mucosa-Associated Lymphatic Tissue).
    • Specific examples: BALT (Bronchus-Associated), GALT (Gut-Associated).
  • Lymphatic Nodules (Follicles): Dense, compact, but non-encapsulated aggregates of lymphocytes.
    • Locations: Walls of the pharynx (tonsils), wall of the ileum (Peyer's Patches), appendix.
üü£ ¬∑ Lymphatic Organs: Structures that are surrounded by a connective tissue capsule, separating them from neighboring tissues.
  • Primary Lymphatic Organs: Site where lymphocytes are produced and mature.
    • Red Bone Marrow: Site of lymphopoiesis (production of lymphocytes). B-lymphocytes (B-cells) and NK cells mature here.
    • Thymus: Site where T-lymphocytes (T-cells) mature.
  • Secondary Lymphatic Organs: Site where most immune responses occur; populated by mature, naive lymphocytes that encounter antigens.
    • Lymph Nodes
    • Spleen

‚ú® Lymphatic Vessels: Detailed Structure & Flow ‚ú®

The lymphatic vessels are found closely associated with blood vessels.

üíß 1. Lymphatic Capillaries (Terminal Lymphatics)

  • Location: Found everywhere in the body except for avascular tissues (CNS, cartilage, cornea, bone, bone marrow, epidermis).
  • Function: Collect interstitial fluid. In the GI tract, these specialized capillaries are called LACTEALS.
  • Structure: Highly permeable and unique compared to blood capillaries:
    • Blind-ended "pockets."
    • Larger diameter for less resistance.
    • Thinner walls with little to no basement membrane.
    • Endothelial cells overlap to form one-way minivalves. Fluid pressure from outside pushes the flap open; pressure from inside pushes it closed, preventing backflow.
    • Anchoring filaments tether the endothelial cells to surrounding connective tissue. This prevents the capillaries from collapsing when interstitial pressure rises.
üìä Figure 3: Lymphatic Capillary Structure
Figure 3: Lymphatic capillary showing blind-ended structure, overlapping endothelial cells (red), one-way valves (green), and anchoring filaments (gray) to prevent collapse

ü©ª 2. Collecting Vessels

  • Receive lymph from the capillaries.
  • Run together with arteries and veins, bound in the same CT sheath.
  • Have the same three tunics as veins (tunica intima, media, adventitia) but with thinner walls and more internal valves. The valves give the vessels a beaded appearance.
  • Superficial Lymphatics: Subcutaneous (in skin) and in areolar CT of mucous and serous membranes.
  • Deep Lymphatics: Larger vessels that accompany deep arteries and veins, supplying internal structures.

üîÑ 3. Lymphatic Trunks & Ducts

Formed where large collecting vessels converge.

üîπ Key Trunks:

  • Lumbar Trunks (Right & Left): Drain lower limbs, pelvic viscera, and kidneys.
  • Intestinal Trunk: Drains the digestive organs. It often joins with the lumbar trunks to form the Cisterna Chyli (a dilated sac at the inferior end of the thoracic duct).
  • Bronchomediastinal, Subclavian, and Jugular Trunks (Right & Left): Drain deep thoracic structures, upper limbs, and head/neck.

üîπ Lymphatic Ducts:

üìä Figure 4: Thoracic Duct and Right Lymphatic Duct
Figure 4: Thoracic duct (left, teal) drains entire body below diaphragm and left side; Right lymphatic duct (red) drains right upper quadrant
‚¨ÖÔ∏è Thoracic Duct (Left Lymphatic Duct):
  • Origin: Arises from the Cisterna Chyli (at L1/L2).
  • Pathway: Drains lymph from the entire body below the diaphragm, plus the left side of the head/neck/thorax and left upper limb.
  • Termination: Empties into the junction of the left internal jugular and left subclavian vein.
‚û°Ô∏è Right Lymphatic Duct:
  • Formation: Formed by the union of the right jugular, right subclavian, and right bronchomediastinal trunks.
  • Drainage: Drains lymph from the right side of the head/neck/thorax and right upper limb.
  • Termination: Empties into the junction of the right internal jugular and right subclavian vein.

‚ú® The Mechanics of Lymph Flow (The "Pumps") ‚ú®

Lymph is a low-pressure fluid. Its movement relies on external forces:

  1. Skeletal Muscle Pump: Contraction of skeletal muscles squeezes the lymphatic vessels.
  2. Respiratory Pump: Pressure changes in the thoracic and abdominal cavities during breathing compress and draw lymph upward.
  3. Pulse Pressure: The pulsation of nearby arteries compresses the lymphatic vessels.
  4. Smooth Muscle in Vessels: The walls of larger lymphatic vessels contain smooth muscle that contracts rhythmically when stretched (intrinsic pump).
  5. One-Way Valves: Ensure lymph flows only toward the subclavian veins.

‚ú® Lymphocytes: The Key Players ‚ú®

We'll go through this in more detail when we cover the immune system. For now, recall that lymphocytes are the "soldiers" of the immune system.

üî¥ T-lymphocytes (T-cells): (~80% of circulating lymphocytes)
  • Mature in: Thymus.
  • Function: Responsible for cell-mediated immunity. They directly attack infected or abnormal cells. They are antigen-specific.
üîµ B-lymphocytes (B-cells): (~10-15% of circulating lymphocytes)
  • Mature in: Red Bone Marrow.
  • Function: Responsible for humoral immunity. Upon activation, they differentiate into plasma cells that produce and secrete antibodies (immunoglobulins). They are antigen-specific.
üü¢ Natural Killer (NK) Cells: (~5-10% of circulating lymphocytes)
  • Mature in: Red Bone Marrow.
  • Function: Part of the innate immune system. They attack foreign cells, virus-infected cells, and cancer cells. They are not antigen-specific.

üîÑ Lymphopoiesis & Circulation:

  • Lymphopoiesis: Production of lymphocytes from hematopoietic stem cells in the red bone marrow.
    • Some stem cells remain in the bone marrow to produce B-cells and NK cells.
    • Other lymphoid stem cells migrate to the thymus to mature into T-cells.
  • Recirculation: Mature, naive lymphocytes constantly travel through the blood, lymph, and lymphatic tissues/organs, "patrolling" for antigens.
  • Proliferation: When T and B-cells are activated by a specific antigen, they proliferate rapidly in the peripheral tissues.
  • Memory Cells: Long-lived cells produced after an initial immune response. They provide long-term immunity (most last up to 4 years, some can last 20+ years).

‚ú® Lymphoid Nodules (Non-Encapsulated) ‚ú®

These are loose CT dominated by lymphocytes and macrophages. They are not organs because they lack a CT capsule.

üëÑ Tonsils:

Large nodules in a ring around the pharynx (Waldeyer's tonsillar ring) to intercept inhaled and ingested pathogens.

  • Pharyngeal tonsil (adenoids) ‚Äì nasopharynx.
  • Palatine tonsils (pair) ‚Äì posterolateral oral cavity. (These are the ones most commonly referred to as "tonsils").
  • Lingual tonsils (pair) ‚Äì base of tongue.
  • Disorder: Infection and swelling is called tonsillitis.

üìç Peyer's Patches:

Aggregated lymphoid nodules in the wall of the ileum (small intestine).

üìç Appendix:

A tubular offshoot of the cecum containing a high concentration of lymphoid nodules.

üìå Note:

Peyer's Patches and the appendix are collectively called GALT (Gut-Associated Lymphoid Tissue).

‚ú® Lymphatic Organs (Encapsulated) ‚ú®

The lymphatic organs are the lymph nodes, thymus, and spleen.

üîç 1. Lymph Nodes (The "Filters")

üéØ Function:

To cleanse lymph and facilitate immune surveillance by activating T & B-cells.

üèóÔ∏è Structure:

  • CT Capsule: Dense connective tissue surrounds the organ.
  • Trabeculae: Capsular fibers extend inward, providing internal support.
  • Hilus: The indented region on one side where blood vessels and nerves enter, and efferent lymphatic vessels exit.
  • Afferent Lymphatic Vessels: Enter on the convex side (opposite the hilus). There are more afferent than efferent vessels, which slows lymph flow and builds pressure, forcing it through the node tissue.

üîÑ Internal Anatomy & Flow:

  1. Lymph enters via afferent vessels -> flows into the subcapsular sinus.
  2. Cortex (Outer): Contains B-cells organized into follicles, some with germinal centers (where B-cells proliferate upon activation).
  3. Paracortex (Deep Cortex): Contains T-cells and dendritic cells.
  4. Medulla (Inner): Contains B-cells, macrophages, and plasma cells in medullary cords.
  5. Lymph seeps through medullary sinuses -> exits at the hilus via efferent vessels.
üìä Figure 8: Lymph Node Internal Structure
Figure 8: Lymph node structure showing capsule, cortex (B-cell follicles with germinal centers), paracortex (T-cells), medulla, and sinuses with afferent/efferent vessels

ü¶† Cellular Action:

Fixed macrophages line the sinuses and phagocytize microbes, debris, and dead cells. Dendritic cells present antigens to T-cells. If an antigen is detected, an immune response is initiated.

üìç Major Clusters:

Lymph nodes are clustered in strategic areas to filter lymph before it returns to blood. Key groups include:

  • Cervical nodes (head/neck)
  • Axillary nodes (breast, upper limb) - critical for breast cancer staging
  • Inguinal nodes (lower limb, genitals)
  • Mesenteric nodes (along the digestive tract)
  • Tracheobronchial nodes (around airways to lungs)
‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Note: When someone says they have "swollen glands", they are technically wrong. They have swollen lymph nodes (lymphadenitis).

üå± 2. Thymus (The "Training Ground")

üìç Location:

Superior mediastinum, posterior to the sternum and anterior to the heart.

üéØ Function:

Site of T-cell maturation. It is most active in children up until about age 2, and then gradually undergoes involution (atrophy), being replaced by adipose and areolar tissue.

üèóÔ∏è Structure:

  • CT Capsule: Extends inward to form two lobes, and then finer septae that divide the lobes into lobules.
  • Lobules: Each contains an outer cortex and inner medulla.
üìä Figure 9: Thymus - Lobules with Cortex and Medulla
Figure 9: Thymus structure showing capsule, septae dividing into lobules, each with outer cortex (red, T-cell proliferation) and inner medulla (teal, T-cell maturation)

üî¨ Histology & Function:

  • The stroma (support structure) is made of reticular epithelial cells (not reticular fibers), which are unique to the thymus.
  • Cortex: Site of rapid T-cell proliferation and positive selection (testing if they can recognize self-MHC). Immature T-cells here are protected from blood-borne antigens by the blood-thymus barrier.
  • Medulla: Site of negative selection (testing if they react to self-antigens). T-cells that pass both tests mature here. They then leave the thymus via medullary blood vessels to seed other lymphatic tissues.
  • Hormones: Reticular epithelial cells secrete thymic hormones (thymosin, thymopoietin, thymulin), which are essential for the maturation process.

ü©∏ 3. Spleen (The "Blood Filter")

üìç Location:

Left, lateral to the stomach, protected by ribs 9-11.

üéØ Function:

  • Filters Blood: Cleanses blood of aged cells, platelets, and debris.
  • Immune Response: Initiates immune responses against blood-borne antigens.
  • Iron Recycling: Macrophages phagocytize old RBCs and recycle the iron (Fe++) for new hemoglobin synthesis.
  • Hematopoiesis: Site of blood cell production in the fetus; can resume this role in adults during extreme anemia (extramedullary hematopoiesis).
  • Blood Reservoir: Stores platelets and some RBCs.
üìä Figure 10: Spleen - White Pulp and Red Pulp
Figure 10: Spleen showing white pulp (blue, lymphocytes around central arteries) and red pulp (pink, venous sinusoids for RBC filtration)

üèóÔ∏è Structure & Circulation:

  • CT Capsule & Trabeculae: Similar to lymph nodes.
  • Hilus: Where splenic artery and vein connect.
  • White Pulp: Lymphocytes and macrophages surrounding central arteries (looks like white islands on a slide). This is where immune surveillance happens.
  • Red Pulp: Consists of venous sinusoids and splenic cords (cords of Billroth) containing RBCs and macrophages. This is where old RBCs are removed.

üîÑ Pathway:

Splenic artery -> trabecular arteries -> central arteries (surrounded by white pulp) -> capillaries -> red pulp (cords & sinusoids) -> trabecular veins -> splenic vein.

‚ö†Ô∏è Clinical Note: Enlargement is called splenomegaly (caused by mono, TB, leukemia). A ruptured spleen causes life-threatening internal hemorrhage and is often removed (splenectomy). You can live without a spleen (functions are taken over by the liver and red bone marrow), but the individual has a higher risk for bacterial infection.
